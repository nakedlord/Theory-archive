## Source Summary

The user asked whether research suggests women become more prone to infidelity after childbirth. The conversation initially reviewed postpartum sexuality and relationship factors, then the user rejected a moral/psychological framing and asked to abstract toward animal behavior and biological strategy.

The durable idea emerged when the user clarified the target hypothesis:

```text
A female who already has a child by one male may become interested in having a child by another male in order to diversify reproductive bets and increase the probability that at least one child is highly successful.
```

The user then requested research directions with reduced influence from cultural, moral, and religious monogamy norms, and added an observational trigger: women with small children appear to show interest toward the user more often than expected.

## Captured Hypothesis Field

The source should be treated as a non-canon working hypothesis about reproductive strategy, not as a conclusion about actual individual women.

Core abstraction:

```text
childbirth does not necessarily end mate scanning;
it may retune mate scanning from pure sexual evaluation toward genetic, protective, resource, coalition, and future-partner evaluation.
```

Strong version:

```text
After producing one child from male A, female strategy may favor a next child from male B as offspring bet-hedging / genetic diversification.
```

Weaker and more defensible version:

```text
After childbirth, female evaluation of males may remain active but shift criteria: candidate males are screened for danger, protection, care, resources, status, genetic value, and possible mate-switching value for the next reproductive decision.
```

## Important Distinctions

1. Increased social attention toward other males is not identical to sexual intent.
2. Interest toward a male around a child may be a safety/resource/coalition scan rather than a reproductive invitation.
3. The child can change the social scene: adults may become more salient, more approachable, or less threatening around a child.
4. A personal observation can be a hypothesis trigger, but not evidence by itself.
5. Human postpartum fertility, lactation, long childhood, paternal investment, and social costs strongly constrain any simple animal-model translation.
