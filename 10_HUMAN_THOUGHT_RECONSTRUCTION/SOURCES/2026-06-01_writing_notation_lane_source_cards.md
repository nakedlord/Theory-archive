## Purpose

These source cards support the Human Thought Reconstruction writing/notation
case:

```text
token -> envelope/impression -> tablet -> abstract numeral / phonetic sign
```

The lane is used to test whether notation gives a bounded analogue of the
stone-tool operator:

```text
future-operation preparation
```

The target is narrower than "the origin of writing":

```text
which operations and distinctions become recoverable when quantities, goods,
names, and administrative states are externalized into durable signs?
```

## Source Card 1: Schmandt-Besserat Token Sequence

Citation:

- Denise Schmandt-Besserat, "Tokens and Writing: the Cognitive Development",
  2013.
- URL:
  https://sites.utexas.edu/dsb/tokens/tokens-and-writing-the-cognitive-development/

Related pages:

- "The Evolution of Writing":
  https://sites.utexas.edu/dsb/tokens/the-evolution-of-writing/
- "From Accounting to Writing":
  https://sites.utexas.edu/dsb/tokens/from-accounting-to-writing/

Used for:

- the proposed Near Eastern sequence from concrete counting to token abstraction,
  envelope/impression practices, written signs, abstract numerals, and phonetic
  signs;
- the idea that tokens and early tablets can be treated as cognitive
  archaeology evidence for abstraction and numeracy;
- the distinction between three-dimensional manipulable counters and
  two-dimensional marks.

Supports:

```text
token system as an external accounting/counting device;
marks as visible proxies for tokens/goods;
writing/notation as a carrier transition from object-handling to sign-handling
```

Does not support:

- a universal theory of all writing origins;
- an uncontested direct path from all tokens to writing;
- whole-culture cognition from administrative artifacts alone.

HTR confidence:

```text
supported for Schmandt-Besserat's model and many concrete mechanisms;
contested for strong origin claims;
useful as operation-lane source if bounded
```

## Source Card 2: Archaic Bookkeeping / Proto-Cuneiform Administration

Citation:

- Hans J. Nissen, Peter Damerow, Robert K. Englund, "Archaic Bookkeeping:
  Early Writing and Techniques of Economic Administration in the Ancient Near
  East", University of Chicago Press, 1993.
- Google Books:
  https://books.google.com/books/about/Archaic_Bookkeeping.html?id=YBAzXV4YtQ8C
- CDLI publication entry:
  https://cdli.earth/publications/1657148

Used for:

- earliest proto-cuneiform as administrative and numerical before it is
  primarily language-recording;
- early tablets as economic bookkeeping devices;
- the need to analyze numerical/metrological systems and administrative
  procedures, not only signs as pictures.

Supports:

```text
early writing as administrative state-management and numerical notation
```

Does not support:

- treating early writing as literature or general speech transcription from the
  beginning;
- assuming the tablet already encodes ordinary language in the later sense;
- reducing all early signs to simple pictures.

HTR confidence:

```text
supported for administrative/numerical record layer;
highly useful for separating notation from full language writing
```

## Source Card 3: Damerow Historical Epistemology

Citation:

- Peter Damerow, "The Origins of Writing as a Problem of Historical
  Epistemology", Max Planck Institute for the History of Science Preprint 114,
  1999.
- PDF:
  https://www.mpiwg-berlin.mpg.de/Preprints/P114.PDF

Used for:

- treating writing and arithmetic origins as historical epistemology rather
  than a simple invention story;
- caution that early writing research must distinguish philological,
  archaeological, administrative, and cognitive questions;
- the idea that abstraction changes through material and administrative
  practices.

Supports:

```text
notation as historically situated abstraction practice
```

Does not support:

- a simple universal abstraction ladder;
- direct inference from signs to mental concepts;
- flattening writing, number, administration, and language into one event.

HTR confidence:

```text
method support / caution source
```

## Source Card 4: CDLI Corpus Boundary

Citation:

- Cuneiform Digital Library Initiative, "About CDLI".
- URL: https://www.cdli.network/about

Used for:

- the existence of a large recoverable corpus of cuneiform inscriptions from the
  beginning of writing and later periods;
- the fact that many early archives are difficult and require specialist
  documentation, transliteration, and interpretation.

Supports:

```text
writing creates durable, inspectable artifact corpora for later reconstruction
```

Does not support:

- direct interpretation of a tablet without philological context;
- assuming a tablet's form alone reveals its cognitive function.

HTR confidence:

```text
supported for corpus/durability premise;
not a source for individual origin claims
```

## Source Card 5: Criticism Of Strong Token-Origin Claims

Citation:

- Paul Zimansky, review of Denise Schmandt-Besserat, "Before Writing", Journal
  of Field Archaeology 20(4), 513-517, 1993.
- PDF copy:
  https://urkesh.org/attach/Zimansky1993.pdf
- Piotr Michalowski, "Tokenism", review article of Schmandt-Besserat 1992,
  American Anthropologist 95, 996-999, 1993.
- Abstract page:
  https://urkesh.org/01a/abstracts/923Michalowski1951.htm

Used for:

- preventing HTR from accepting the token-to-writing model as uncontested;
- warning that token classification, geographic/chronological spread, and direct
  token-to-sign continuity are disputed;
- preserving the difference between a useful operation model and a settled
  origin story.

Supports:

```text
token path should be treated as contested and bounded
```

Does not support:

- rejecting all accounting/notation links;
- rejecting the usefulness of tokens and tablets as cognitive artifacts;
- treating criticism as closure of the question.

HTR confidence:

```text
strong caution source;
keeps the case at method_probe / non-canon
```

## Combined Source Boundary

Allowed:

- use the token/envelope/impression/tablet sequence as a bounded Near Eastern
  administrative notation lane;
- treat marks and tablets as external memory/accounting surfaces;
- infer count-object, sign-token, and record-event distinctions cautiously;
- compare notation with stone-tool preparation at the level of operator logic.

Not allowed:

- claim that all writing derives from tokens;
- claim that early tablets were primarily speech transcription;
- infer whole mental states from sign form;
- erase institutional, economic, and power contexts;
- turn the sequence into a universal progress ladder.
